üí™üèæAdvantages of Using Satellite Images for Land Area Estimation:

  • Large-Scale Coverage:

    • Satellites can capture imagery of vast areas quickly and consistently. This is particularly beneficial for estimating land area in remote or inaccessible regions.

  • Timely Updates:

    • Modern satellites revisit locations frequently, allowing for regular monitoring of land cover changes and updates to land area estimates.

  • Cost-Effectiveness:

    • Freely available satellite imagery from sources like Landsat and Sentinel programs offers a cost-efficient solution compared to traditional ground surveys.

  • Objectivity:

    • Satellite imagery provides a consistent and objective perspective on land cover, minimizing human error and bias present in field measurements.

  • Spectral Information:

    • Satellites capture data beyond the visible spectrum, allowing for the creation of specialized indices like NDWI (Normalized Difference Water Index) to accurately distinguish water bodies from land.

‚è∫Ô∏è Limitations of Using Satellite Images for Land Area Estimation:

  • Resolution Limitations:

    • While improving, satellite image resolution might not capture very small features or detailed land cover changes. This can lead to underestimation or overestimation of land area, particularly for complex landscapes.

  • Cloud Cover:

    • Clouds can obstruct the ground view, hindering accurate land cover classification and potentially leading to inaccurate land area estimates. Cloud filtering techniques help, but may exclude valuable data.

  • Atmospheric Conditions:

    • Atmospheric haze and dust can affect the spectral properties of land cover, making it challenging to distinguish land from water or other features.

  • Tidal Influence:

    • Tidal variations can impact water boundaries, especially in coastal regions. This can introduce errors in land area estimates if not taken into account.

üåè We utilize freely available, high-resolution (10m) Sentinel-2 imagery within Google Earth Engine (GEE).

Focusing on a specific year, we meticulously filter for minimal cloud cover (e.g., less than 10%) to ensure data quality.

The Normalized Difference Water Index (NDWI) then separates land from water using spectral bands sensitive to water absorption and reflectance.

A binary water mask is created using a threshold on NDWI, and the Canny edge detection algorithm refines water body boundaries for precise delineation.

This methodology offers a rapid, cost-effective solution for tracking annual land cover changes, with future versions exploring even more robust approaches for the vector layer.

Methodology:
  1. Data Acquisition and Filtering:
    • üõ∞Ô∏è Access freely available satellite imagery collections like Landsat or Sentinel-2 within GEE.
    • Filter the imagery for a specific year by date range.
    • Apply cloud cover filtering to ensure high-quality data (e.g., less than 10% cloud cover).
  2. Water Index Calculation:
    • Calculate the Normalized Difference Water Index (NDWI) to distinguish water bodies from land. NDWI utilizes specific spectral bands (e.g., B8 and B3) sensitive to water absorption and reflectance.
  3. Water Mask Creation and Edge Detection:
    • Apply a threshold to the NDWI to create a binary water mask, where water pixels are assigned a specific value.
    • Implement the Canny edge detection algorithm directly on the water mask to precisely delineate water boundaries.
  4. Land Area Calculation üíª
    • Export the Water Land Boundary to a vector layer.
    • Generate the outermost boundary of the country.
    • Convert the boundary to a vector polygon.
    • Manually remove all inland water bodies from within the country boundary.
    • Generalize the boundary line for mapping purposes (accuracy addressed in future versions).
